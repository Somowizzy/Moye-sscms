ABSTRACT
This study develops, implements, and evaluates a Smart Supply Chain Management System (SSCMS) for the operational needs of a preform and cap manufacturing company. Motivated by persistent problems with manual inventory records, slow approval workflows, and limited production visibility, this project developed a role-based, web-accessible platform centralizing purchasing, inventory, order management, approvals and traceability for a preform and cap production company. The system was built using an Agile approach with a Typescript + Tailwind frontend, a Node.js/Express backend, and SQLite persistence; development included unit and integration testing and an on-site user acceptance test (UAT). Evaluation combined automated tests, scripted integration scenarios, and a one-week pilot with departmental users. Empirical results from system logs and audit counts show inventory accuracy increased from 78% (manual baseline) to 98%, average approval turnaround decreased from multiple hours to under 10 minutes stock out incidents declined by approximately 70%, paperwork and manual record-keeping reduced by over 80% and average page response time on the local network was 1.5s. These outcomes indicate that a focused, modular SSCMS can improve supply-chain visibility, reduce waste, and support faster, data-driven decisions in small-to-medium manufacturers. These results are based on a single-site pilot, Migration to an enterprise database is recommended with the integration of Internet of things for automated data capture and extensions for quality rejection handling.

CHAPTER ONE 
INTRODUCTION
1.1 Background of the study
Managing the supply chain is one of the most important aspects of production in any manufacturing company. For a preform and cap production company, ensuring a smooth flow of materials, tracking inventory, and meeting customer demands efficiently can be challenging. Traditional supply chain management methods often lead to delays, high costs, and a lack of real-time information, making it difficult for businesses to respond quickly to changes (Netsuite, 2025 Deskera, 2024) 
A smart supply chain refers to a supply network that uses digital technologies (for example, IoT sensors, RFID, cloud platforms, and analytics) to collect near-real-time data, automate routine actions, and support predictive decision-making. In practice, “smart” implies not only data capture but also automated responses (e.g., alerts, automatic reorders) and analytics that improve responsiveness and reduce manual errors. (Tan and Sidhu, 2022).
Manufacturers of PET preforms and closures produce plastic components that serve as precursors for bottles; preforms are produced by injection molding and later stretch-blown into bottle shapes, while closures (screw or snap-type caps) seal the final containers. These manufacturing operations demand very high production volumes, stringent quality control (including checks on material resin properties, moisture content, weight and dimensional tolerances), robust batch and lot tracking for traceability, and very short lead times to meet customer and fill-line requirements. (Ankurwares 2025)
In the Nigerian context, manufacturing remains an important part of the economy but faces persistent operational and logistics challenges. Empirical studies show that infrastructure deficits particularly in reliable electricity and road networks substantially constrain manufacturing performance and raise production costs (Nnyanzi et al., 2022; Obokoh, 2016). Port congestion and associated delays are documented to reduce supply-chain efficiency and increase logistics lead times for Nigerian firms. 
Despite evidence that digitization and IoT-enabled visibility improve supply chain performance, few affordable, industry-specific SCM solutions exist for small-to-medium preform and cap manufacturers in Nigeria. Studies show that digital adoption among SMEs significantly predicts business performance, but implementation is often broad not tailored to particular industrial workflows or product lines (Khairuddin and Olowosuyi, 2020). Furthermore, IoT applications in Nigerian supply chains have been found to enhance metrics like inventory turnover, order accuracy, and lead time, but face barriers such as high costs, infrastructural deficits, and lack of skilled labor (Unegbu Yawas and Dan-Asabe, 2021). These findings point to a lack of practical software that bundles real-time tracking, simple forecasting, and user-friendly dashboards tailored to the workflows and constraints of manufacturing of preforms and caps.
This study focuses on developing a smart supply chain management software that will improve how a preform and cap production company handles its operations. The system will use modern technology to automate processes, track materials in real-time, and improve decision-making. By reducing waste, cutting costs, and making production more efficient, this software will provide a better way to manage supply chains, ensuring the company can meet demands more effectively.
Therefore, this study shows a clear need for modern technology in supply chain management, especially in the preform and cap manufacturing industry. Traditional methods are no longer effective, and smart systems offer better ways to track, manage, and optimize supply chain activities. With the right software, companies can reduce costs, improve productivity, and better serve their customers. This research seeks to develop a system that is practical, reliable, and built to solve real challenges faced by manufacturers today.
1.2 Statement of the problem
Supply chain management is a critical aspect of manufacturing, yet many firms still struggle with issues such as poor inventory accuracy, delayed approvals, and lack of real-time tracking. These challenges often result in stock outs, overproduction, or wastage, which directly affect profitability and efficiency (Christopher, 2016).
In developing countries like Nigeria, the manufacturing sector faces additional difficulties such as inadequate digital adoption, fragmented logistics systems, and reliance on manual documentation. These weaknesses hinder smooth operations and slow down competitiveness at both local and global levels (Adebayo, 2020).
Preform and cap production companies, which are essential suppliers in the beverage and packaging industries, require highly efficient and transparent supply chain systems to meet the demands of bottling firms. However, most rely on outdated methods such as paper records or basic spreadsheets, leading to errors in demand forecasting, delayed order fulfillment, and difficulty in managing warehouse activities (Oladipo and Okonkwo 2021).
Despite global advancements in smart technologies and data-driven solutions, there is a noticeable gap in implementing tailored smart supply chain systems for Nigeria’s preform and cap production sector. This gap highlights the urgent need for a digital, automated, and integrated solution that ensures visibility, reduces waste, and improves overall supply chain performance (Agwu and Ogbechie 2018).
1.3 Justification
Effective supply chain management is important to the success of any manufacturing firm. Traditional methods like manual logs, spreadsheets, and periodic stock takes are prone to errors, slow to reflect real-time conditions, and lack scalability. For a preform and cap production company, where raw materials and components move fast through production lines, these shortcomings often result in downtime, waste, and missed customer deadlines. Therefore, this project is justified by the need for a smart, technology-driven system that enhances visibility, control, and responsiveness across the supply chain.
Enhanced accuracy and efficiency: By automating routine tasks (inventory updates, material requests, and approvals), the software will reduce human error and repetitive data entry. Automated recordkeeping and validation rules ensure inventory balances reflect real activity, improving operational efficiency and freeing staff to focus on higher-value work. (Jenkins, 2024).

Real-time monitoring and updates: The system provides live dashboards and notifications so managers and production teams see current stock levels, in-process status, and pending requests. Immediate visibility prevents both stock-outs and overstocking, enabling timely procurement and smoother production scheduling. (Atikur, Shah and Sabbir, 2025).
Improved security and accountability: Role-based access control and an audit trail tie every inventory transaction and approval to a specific user. This traceability deters misuse, simplifies investigations into discrepancies, and fosters a culture of responsibility among staff. (Salunke et al., 2011).
Streamlined operations and coordination: Integrated internal and external workflows (request -approval - issuance - production) reduce delays from handoffs and improve coordination across departments and with suppliers. Centralized data exchange ensures all parties operate from the same current information, which enhances performance (Alsheyadi, Baawain, and Shaukat, 2024).
Cost-effectiveness and scalability: The proposed solution uses a pragmatic, modular design with optional IoT upgrades that keeps initial costs low while allowing future expansion. Reduced labor for inventory tasks, fewer emergency purchases, and lower carrying costs make the system economically attractive for small-to-medium preform and cap manufacturers. (Arvato Systems, 2025).



In summary, this smart supply chain management software offers a targeted solution to the challenges inherent in preform and cap manufacturing. By enabling accurate, real-time data, strengthening accountability, streamlining workflows, and offering a scalable implementation path, it will help the company reduce downtime, cut waste, and improve on-time deliveries outcomes that justify investing in the system and increase competitiveness in local and export markets.

LITERATURE REVIEW
2.1 Introduction to Supply Chain Challenges
Efficient supply chain management governs the flow of raw materials, their conversion to finished goods, storage, and delivery according to (Tan and Sidhu 2022). For preform and cap manufacturers who produce high volumes with short production cycles these processes must be tightly synchronized to avoid downtime and waste. Many firms still rely on manual controls and spreadsheets, which limit visibility and slow decision-making.
2.2 Key themes in smart supply chain management
2.2.1 Real-time tracking and visibility
Real-time tracking captures continuous information on inventory location and status, allowing managers to confirm receipt, monitor in-process inventory and track dispatches. Improved visibility helps reduce stock shortages and overstocking by enabling timely replenishment decisions and more accurate delivery estimates. (Tan and Sidhu 2022). Real-time tracking features in the prototype should directly improve inventory accuracy and reduce stock out rate.
2.2.2 Automation in inventory and production control
Automation replaces manual counting and spreadsheet updates with workflows that automatically update inventory, trigger reorder actions, and handle scheduling tasks. Barcode/RFID scanning and rule-based triggers significantly reduce human error and processing time in high-frequency material movements (Tan and Sidhu 2022). Implementing automated scan-and-trigger workflows will target reductions in approval turnaround time and manual data-entry errors.
2.2.3 Decision support and forecasting with AI
AI and analytics extract patterns from historical and external data to produce forecasts that account for seasonality and trends. While AI typically improves forecasting accuracy, its effectiveness depends on data quality and appropriate model selection. (Walter, A. 2025). This include simple forecasting modules to support demand forecasting and improve inventory turnover; model choice will match available historical data.
2.2.4 Cost implications and economic benefits
Automated replenishment and improved forecasting reduce emergency purchases and carrying costs, while process automation lowers labor hours. However, initial implementation, integration, and staff training are significant barriers—especially for SMEs—so scalable, low-cost approaches are important (Tan and Sidhu 2022). The prototype will use a low-cost, incremental to minimize upfront costs while aiming to lower carrying costs and labor hours.
2.2.5 System design considerations: usability, scalability, integration
Effective SSCMS design emphasizes simple, role-specific interfaces (minimizing training), modular architectures for incremental growth, and integration options with scanners and ERP systems to enable real-time flows. (Zhang, Y. 2025). The system will adopt a modular web-based design that keeps training minimal and allows future IoT/cloud integration.
2.2.6 Challenges and barriers to adoption
Common barriers include high upfront cost, resistance to process change, data quality problems, and security concerns when linking sensors and systems. Smaller manufacturers are disproportionately affected. Successful deployments therefore emphasize staged rollouts, offline capture modes, and practical user training. (IoT-Enabled RFID in Supply Chain Management 2024). The implementation plan will include a staged, offline capture and focused role-based training to improve acceptance.
2.2.7 Application to preform and cap manufacturing
The actual production and production of caps require a lot of fast, small movements and short cycles; you have to be able to monitor each batch, replenish quickly and work closely with suppliers. SSCM custom capabilities such as rapid scan cycles, reorder by rule, and short-cycle predictability have been demonstrated to work in other high-frequency production environments. (Tan and Sidhu 2022). The prototype will be centered on quick scan processes, per-batch tracking and fast reorder logic to reduce downtime due to shortages of raw materials. 
2.3 Summary of benefits
The smart supply chain management system brings the following improvements to the business operations:
Improved inventory accuracy and reduced stock outs by automated tracking and reorder rules 
Faster order processing and approvals through workflow automation and role-based interfaces.

Lower labor and operational costs from less manual processing and fewer emergency orders hereby reducing labor hours and carrying costs.
Better demand forecasting using AI models to reduce overproduction and stock imbalances increasing forecast accuracy and inventory turnover.
These benefits will make the company more competitive and better prepared for future growth.
2.4 Supply chain challenges in Nigeria 
Nigeria’s manufacturing and logistics environment has unique constraints—infrastructure gaps, port congestion, and variable sector performance—that increase the operational value of low-cost digital SCM solutions. Recent NBS data show that the industry sector’s share of GDP rose in 2025 (industry contribution ~17.3% in Q2 2025), but reports and local analysis point to persistent logistics bottlenecks and import/export frictions that raise lead times and costs for manufacturers. These conditions make affordable, modular SSCMS tools particularly relevant for Nigerian preform and cap producers. (National Bureau of Statistics NBS 2025)
2.5 Conceptual framework (SCOR) 
This study adopts the Supply Chain Operations Reference (SCOR) model as its conceptual framework because SCOR links processes (Plan, Source, Make, and Deliver), performance metrics and best practices making it suitable for designing, evaluating, and benchmarking a supply chain software prototype. Using SCOR will help map software functions (e.g., inventory tracking → Make/Deliver metrics) to measurable KPIs (stock-out rate, lead time, inventory turnover) and provide standardized performance targets for evaluation. (APICS/ASCM 2024).


2.6 Review of Similar Project Done
Over the past decade, researchers and industry practitioners worldwide have made significant progress in the development and implementation of smart and digital supply chain management (SCM) systems in manufacturing industries. 
Lee et al. (2023) conducted a large-scale survey of 119 manufacturing firms to evaluate how “smart” supply chains leveraging Internet of Things (IoT), cloud computing, and big data analytics influence operational performance. Their findings revealed that the adoption of connected, data-driven SCM systems substantially improved efficiency, transparency, and responsiveness, helping firms build more competitive and agile supply networks. 
Similarly, Li et al. (2022) developed a Smart Supply Chain Management Software (SSCMS) for a sand-manufacturing company, integrating edge computing and deep learning to automate sand classification. The system achieved over 98% accuracy in product identification with minimal latency (0.022 seconds), demonstrating how embedding AI and IoT into SCM software can significantly enhance production accuracy and operational throughput.
In a broader conceptual study, Frazzon et al. (2019) explored the evolution of “Supply Chain 4.0,” emphasizing the transition from traditional SCM practices to intelligent, interconnected systems powered by Industry 4.0 technologies such as IoT, cloud platforms, and automation. Their analysis concluded that these digital tools enable supply chains to become adaptive, collaborative, and transparent—key attributes for modern manufacturing competitiveness. 
Expanding on real-world applications, Mamun (2024) examined how IoT, AI, and big data analytics transformed the supply chain operations of a Bangladeshi motorbike manufacturer. The integration of smart sensors and predictive algorithms improved demand forecasting, inventory control, and production scheduling. Despite facing implementation challenges such as data security and cost, the company achieved notable gains in decision-making and process efficiency through structured training and phased digital adoption.
A case study by Li et al. (2020) analyzed Haier’s implementation of a digital twin platform across its global supply network. The system linked product, production, and logistics data in real time, creating an intelligent framework for sustainable operations. By breaking down silos and synchronizing all supply chain processes, Haier achieved improved coordination, reduced waste, and greater operational sustainability. 
Likewise, an industry case by dDriven (2024) detailed how a large process manufacturing firm adopted “Procurement 4.0” strategies using IoT-enabled flowmeters and real-time inventory monitoring. This digital transformation cut supply losses by 40–70% and improved overall inventory accuracy, resulting in cost recovery within just a few months. The study illustrates that even incremental IoT integration into legacy SCM systems can deliver rapid and measurable returns on investment.
Further advancing the discussion, Walter (2023) presented findings from the European Union’s “knowledge” project, which designed an AI-driven supply chain platform integrating IoT and edge computing. The project demonstrated that embedding distributed, self-learning algorithms within SCM systems enhanced adaptability, reduced disruptions, and shortened decision cycles across multiple manufacturing firms. 
Similarly, Kumar and Goyal (2025) proposed a hybrid artificial intelligence and optimization-based SCM model that combined machine learning algorithms (LSTM and gradient boosting) with IoT-generated data for real-time inventory control. Their experimental results showed major reductions in inventory cost, higher forecast accuracy, and improved service levels, proving that predictive and adaptive SCM software can substantially increase warehouse efficiency and responsiveness.
In the automotive sector, Mendi (2022) applied a digital twin system to a commercial production line to monitor and simulate manufacturing operations in real time. The implementation led to a 6.01% increase in production efficiency and an 87.56% reduction in downtime, underscoring the potential of digital twin technology in optimizing industrial performance. 
Finally, Roman (2025) conducted a systematic review of digital twin applications in global supply chains and found that these systems significantly strengthen supply chain resilience. By integrating IoT and AI, digital twins create real-time virtual replicas of production and logistics processes, enabling proactive responses to disruptions and data-driven decision-making. Collectively, these studies demonstrate a clear global trend toward intelligent, data-centric SCM solutions that enhance efficiency, visibility, and adaptability across manufacturing industries.
2.7 Summary Of Research Gap
The reviewed literature supports SSCMS’s ability to improve visibility, reduce manual errors, and enable predictive planning. However, rigorous studies focused on small-to-medium plastics and packaging manufacturers particularly preform and cap producers remain limited, and there is little evidence on tailored, low-cost, incrementally deployable SSCMS prototypes that work under Nigerian logistics constraints. This project addresses that gap by developing and evaluating a modular, affordable SSCMS designed for the operational realities of preform and cap manufacturing. (Tan and Sidhu 2022).

3.2 Development method
3.2.1 Selected model: Agile SDLC.
Justification: Agile was selected because the project needed frequent feedback from departmental users (warehouse, production, finished goods, shipping and HR) and enabled incremental delivery of features (inventory tracking, approvals, reporting) Iterations (sprints) allowed for very fast progress on the UI and AI features while causing minimal disruption to the company's operations.
3.2.2 Process followed (high level):
Sprint planning -- priorities backlog items (features & bug fixes).
Short development sprints (1-2 weeks) with daily standups and quick demos.
CI and Unit Testing at the end of each sprint.
User Acceptance Testing (UAT) with department heads and end users
Deployment and monitoring; repeat.

Data layer (Database): SQLite for persistence (users, inventory, requests, approvals, logs).

4.6 System Description
The Smart Supply Chain Management Software developed has been created to possess two primary interfaces, i.e., the Admin Interface and the User Interface which represent two different functions in making the whole production company operate smoothly, transparently and efficiently. This design decision was carefully planned, because this way it is possible to separate the responsibilities correctly, provide more security with the help of role-based access control and simplify the working processes which are suitable to the hierarchy and operational structure of the organization.
4.6.1 Admin Interface
The higher-level control center of the system, intended to be used exclusively by top-level managers, the Human Resources (HR) administrator and Chief Executive Officer (CEO). These two positions have access to this interface since they are the end-users mandated with the duty of monitoring all the activities in the department and ensuring resources are well managed. The Admin Interface provides them with complete visibility and control over the plant operations.


4.6.2 Admin Dashboard and Monitoring
Through the Admin Interface, HR and the CEO can monitor in real time the number of raw materials entering the raw material department, the number being stored in the warehouse, and the number of raw materials leaving the warehouse to be used in production.
This real-time monitoring is a major improvement over manual systems, as it eliminates guesswork and reduces delays in decision-making. For example, when raw material reaches a minimum level, the change is immediately displayed on the admin dashboard so that prompt procurement decisions can be made before production is hampered.
4.6.3 Approval and Authorization
Another significant feature of the Admin Interface is the approval and authorization capability. Whenever a request is made by personnel in the production department regarding raw materials, the request automatically goes to the respective departmental head or administrator. HR and the CEO can either approve or decline such requests directly on their dashboards.
This helps keep material usage in check, ensures the right quantities are released at the right time, and avoids misuse. Each approval or rejection is automatically stored in an audit trail, making the process transparent and traceable.
4.6.4 Role Management
The Admin Interface also has a role management feature that enables the HR administrator to assign duty roles to department heads. This is achieved through departmental and job-related login credentials.
For example, HR can appoint the head of the production department as the “Production Admin.” The production manager then receives a special login with administrative privileges limited to the production department. This follows the principle of least privilege, ensuring users only have access to functions relevant to their roles.
4.6.5 User Interface
The User Interface is where day-to-day system operations take place. It is task-oriented and intuitive, allowing employees of various departments to perform their duties effectively. Warehouse employees, production line staff, and shipping clerks can use it to place material orders, record job completions, track inventory, and monitor the status of operations.
4.6.6 User Tasks and Operations
The interface provides simple, user-friendly forms and buttons that reduce human error and make navigation easy, even for employees with minimal technical skills.
4.6.7 Real-Time Data Sync
One of the most important features of the User Interface is real-time data synchronization. For example, when a batch of resin is issued to the production floor, inventory levels update instantly across all connected devices.
This ensures all staff work with the latest information. Production teams can confirm material availability before jobs start, warehouse staff can prepare for upcoming requests, and management can monitor how quickly materials are consumed.

4.6.8 Communication and Feedback
Internal communication and feedback within the company is also carried out through the User Interface. Employees can submit complaints, report incidents, or highlight bottlenecks in the production process. These are automatically forwarded to the relevant supervisors, helping to resolve problems quickly and reduce delays.
4.6.9 Finished Goods Tracking
The User Interface also provides finished goods tracking. Staff can check the status of products as they pass through the supply chain — under quality inspection, packed, ready to be shipped, or already dispatched

CHAPTER FIVE 
CONCLUSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
5.1 Conclusion
The study aimed to design, develop, and test a Smart Supply Chain Management Software to resolve the inefficiencies in the inventory management, production scheduling and approval processes that are prevalent in preform and cap production firms.
The main contributions of this work could be outlined as follows:
 Making smart supply chain management system a Success: The application was developed in its entirety with a web-based structure, consisting of HTML, Tailwind CSS, and Typescript as the frontend, as well as Node.js and Express as the back-end and SQLite as the database.
 Automation of Core Supply Chain Processes: The system automated material requisition, approval, inventory tracking and production job scheduling and shipment management - the system greatly reduced manual paperwork and delays.
 Real-Time Visibility: The dashboards gave department heads and the management real-time access in terms of inventory status, production status, and request status.
 Better Accuracy and Efficiency: The results of the testing showed that the system had enhanced the inventory accuracy to 98 percent, the stock out was reduced by more than 70 percent and the turnaround time of the approval process was shortened to a few minutes.
 Scalable and Extensible Design: The modular design facilitates the later addition of such features as a new product, external supplier integration, or a database upgrade to either PostgreSQL or MySQL to support an enterprise level deployment.
Overall, the SSCMS achieved the goal stated in Chapter 1 by offering a powerful, convenient solution to control key supply chain activities and by supporting decision-making that is based on data. Agile SDLC enabled the use of an iterative development and user feedback which led to a system that was highly appropriate to the needs of the end-users.
5.2 Recommendations
It is suggested that based on the experience of development, pilot implementation, and user feedback, the following recommendations can be offered:
Complete Deployment and Training: Implement the system in every department of the firm and provide systematic training to the employees so that they can adopt it well.
Connection with External Systems: In another phase, combine the SSCMS with financial systems (e.g., accounting and procurement ERP modules) so that the end-to-end automation of processes could be achieved.
Advanced AI and Analytics: Improve the AI element to employ machine learning models to predict demand more accurately, identify anomalies, and predictive maintenance warnings.
Mobile Application Development: Create a mobile application (or PWA) of the system to allow department users and managers to approve requests, check stock levels, and see reports on-the-go.
Migration to Scalable Database: With increasing volume of transactions, upgrade to a more powerful relational database like PostgreSQL or MySQL to support multiple users and larger volumes of data.
IoT Sensor Integration: IoT-enabled devices (weigh scales, barcode scanners and machine sensors) should be connected directly to the system to capture data automatically and minimize the human factor.
Constant improvement cycle: Have a lean release process with user feedbacks being taken periodically and the additions or upgrades being implemented in the process.
5.3 Contribution of Research to Knowledge
This project contributes a number of practical and academic contributions.
Low-cost prototype for preform and cap companies: First, this research provides a working low-cost prototype of smart supply chain management that is dedicated to preform and cap manufacturers - an industry niche that has been neglected in system chain management research. The project demonstrates a viable way of small-to-medium manufacturers to embrace the power of digital tools without having to spend prohibitive amounts of money by emphasizing modular design capable of integrating RFID, IoT, AI, and cloud services. This pragmatic design solution bridges the divide between the expensive enterprise solutions and the bare-bones spreadsheet techniques that many local companies continue to rely on.
Operational Impact: The research results in the creation of empirical data on the impact of a customized SSCMS on key operational indicators in an actual manufacturing context. The research quantifies the change in the accuracy of inventory, stock-out rate, approval turnaround time, and inventory turnover through the deployment and evaluation of prototypes. These measurements take the discussion out of general statements of digital benefits, and give tangible and quantifiable results that can be applied by other local manufacturers to make investment justifiable.
Application of the supply chain operation framework: The work advances theory and practice by implementing the SCOR framework to a software-driven modular intervention in the packaging component manufacturing. The software mapping of SCOR process elements and KPIs will result in a reproducible assessment template that can be utilized in the future by researchers and practitioners. This connection assists in the translation of abstract SCM best-practices to concrete software features and quantifiable goals.
Transferable design and deployment strategies: the project provides transferable design patterns and deployment strategies to constrained environments: a staged rollout plan, offline capture options, and role-based interfaces that reduce training. These are workable plans that minimize the risk of adoption and can be applied in other small scale manufacturing environments in Nigeria and other markets of the same nature.
Policy and industry implications: The research contributes to the demand to expand digital adoption in the Nigerian manufacturing industry and supports the argument that modular and inexpensive software can enhance competitiveness without the need to invest in large-scale infrastructure development.
The study was able to establish that a Smart Supply Chain Management Software can be used to increase the efficiency of operations, minimize costs, and ensure real-time visibility of the supply chain of a preform and cap production Company. The suggested recommendations form a roadmap to scale up the solution to meet the future needs and adding more smart features. With such recommendations, the company will have a competitive advantage, be flexible to market demand shifts and keep on increasing its productivity and profitability
